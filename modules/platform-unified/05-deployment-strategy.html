Platform Deployment Strategy

🕒 40 min 📚 4 sections ⭐ 200 XP Level 3 - Advanced

🎯 Learning Objectives

In this module you will cover:

  • Execute a phased rollout that introduces SCA, SAST, Container, and Secrets in the right order
  • Apply organizational change management and team enablement practices
  • Scale from a pilot team to full production across many teams
  • Migrate from point tools to the unified Mend platform without disruption

The Phased Rollout Approach

Adopting four security products at once is a recipe for change-management overload. The proven Mend deployment strategy introduces products sequentially, each phase building on the muscle memory and goodwill established by the previous one. The recommended order is SCA first, then SAST, then Container, then Secrets — chosen because each step delivers visible value quickly while progressively tightening the security net.

①

Phase 1: SCA

Software Composition Analysis is the fastest to value. Most teams have never seen their full dependency tree, so the first scan is an eye-opener. Reachability analysis immediately quiets the noise and builds credibility. Target: 2-4 weeks to first dashboard.

②

Phase 2: SAST

With SCA trusted, add SAST on a single pilot repository. Expect a larger finding volume; use the deduplication and remediation workflows from the integrated platform to keep developer burden manageable. Target: 4-6 weeks to expand across a team.

③

Phase 3: Container

Container scanning extends coverage to the deployable artifact. It often uncovers base-image vulnerabilities and misconfigurations the team did not know existed. Target: 3-5 weeks, often faster because the pipeline integration pattern is already established.

④

Phase 4: Secrets

Secrets detection is last because it is the most sensitive — findings may require immediate credential rotation. By this point the team trusts the platform and has mature remediation workflows. Target: 2-3 weeks to steady state.

💡 Let Value Drive Velocity

Do not fix the phase timeline in stone. If SCA adoption stalls because a team is digesting a large backlog, pause before introducing SAST. The goal of a phased rollout is earned trust, not calendar compliance.

Organizational Change Management & Team Enablement

Technology rollouts fail far more often from people problems than from technical ones. A successful Mend deployment treats enablement as a first-class workstream alongside configuration. Three practices consistently determine outcomes: executive sponsorship, champion networks, and developer-friendly onboarding.

Executive sponsorship gives the program authority and air cover. A visible executive — typically a CISO or VP of Engineering — communicates why the platform matters, protects the program when teams push back, and celebrates wins publicly. Without this, security tooling is easily deprioritized against feature deadlines.

A champion network seeds each engineering team with one developer who is trained deeply on Mend and serves as the first point of contact for colleagues. Champions catch frustration early, translate security jargon into engineering terms, and feed real-world friction back to the platform team. Invest in champions with dedicated training time and recognition; they multiply the reach of a small security team many times over.

💡 The 90-Day Onboarding Curve

Expect roughly 90 days from a team’s first scan to genuine adoption — the point where developers proactively consult Mend findings before merge. Shorten the curve with office hours, a dedicated Slack channel, and a "first fix" celebration that makes the initial remediation experience positive rather than punitive.

Pilot-to-Production Scaling & Point-Tool Migration

Scaling from one pilot team to the whole organization requires repeatable patterns rather than heroics. Mend recommends a landing zone approach: a small set of reference projects, fully configured with policies, integrations, and dashboards, that serve as templates every new team can clone. The landing zone codifies best practices so each onboarding does not reinvent them.

For organizations migrating from existing point tools, a parallel-run period reduces risk. Run Mend alongside the legacy tool for 30-60 days, comparing findings and tuning policies until parity is demonstrated. Then retire the legacy tool with a clear cutover date. Parallel running also surfaces finding gaps — vulnerabilities the old tool missed that Mend catches — which becomes powerful evidence for the migration business case.

  1. Inventory existing tools — Document every point tool in use, its owner, its cost, and the coverage gaps it leaves. This becomes the migration backlog and the baseline for ROI measurement.
  2. Build the landing zone — Configure reference projects with policies, gates, integrations, and dashboards. Validate the configuration with a friendly pilot team.
  3. Migrate team by team — Onboard teams in waves of 3-5, using the landing zone as a template. Provide each wave with a champion, office hours, and a 30-day check-in.
  4. Retire legacy tools — Once a team has run Mend in parallel and confirmed parity, cut over and decommission the legacy tool. Capture the cost savings for the ROI story.

⚠️ Avoid Big-Bang Cutover

Migrating every team on a single weekend maximizes risk and minimizes learning. Wave-based migration lets each wave benefit from the previous wave’s lessons and keeps the support load manageable for the security team running the program.
