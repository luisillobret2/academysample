Cross-Product Policy & Governance

In this module you will cover:

  • Apply policy templates and enforce governance across all Mend products
  • Map security policies to compliance frameworks and produce governance reporting

The Unified Policy Framework

When an organization adopts multiple Mend products in isolation, each one ends up with its own policy settings, its own thresholds, and its own enforcement rules. Security teams then struggle to answer a simple question: "Are we consistently enforcing our risk appetite across the entire software factory?" The Mend unified platform solves this through a cross-product policy framework that lets you define, apply, and audit a single set of governance rules across Software Composition Analysis (SCA), Static Application Security Testing (SAST), Container security, and Secrets detection.

The framework rests on four pillars. First, a shared policy vocabulary uses common severity definitions, risk scoring, and action verbs (block, warn, ignore) so that a "Critical" finding means the same thing whether it comes from a vulnerable dependency or a hardcoded secret. Second, a centralized policy service stores every rule in one place, eliminating the drift that occurs when each product maintains its own configuration database. Third, an enforcement layer translates policies into pipeline gates, IDE warnings, and pull-request comments. Fourth, a governance reporting engine rolls policy outcomes up into compliance evidence and executive dashboards.

💡 Why Cross-Product Policy Matters

Organizations running point tools typically maintain 3 to 5 separate policy consoles. A single application may be governed by conflicting rules — SCA blocks Critical CVEs, but SAST only warns on Critical injection flaws. The unified framework eliminates this inconsistency by letting a security architect define one policy that applies to every product, then override it only where business context demands it.

Hierarchical Policy Inheritance

Mend’s policy model is hierarchical, mirroring how most enterprises structure their software portfolios. Policies flow downward through three levels: Organization, Project, and Repository. A rule defined at the organization level automatically applies to every project and repository beneath it, unless a lower level explicitly overrides it. This dramatically reduces configuration effort while preserving the flexibility to tighten or loosen controls for specific teams.

  1. Organization-level policies — Set the enterprise baseline: block Critical CVEs in production-bound builds, fail any build with a hardcoded secret, require SAST clean on merge to main. These are the non-negotiable guardrails.
  2. Project-level policies — Tighten or relax rules for a group of related repositories. A payments project may block High-severity SAST findings where the org only blocks Critical. A legacy modernization project may temporarily allow warnings instead of blocks.
  3. Repository-level policies — The finest granularity, used for exceptional cases such as a single repo that needs a one-off ignore rule or an experimental branch with reduced enforcement.

💡 Override Discipline

Every override is logged with a reason, an owner, and an expiration date. This prevents "policy rot" where temporary exceptions become permanent. Review overrides quarterly and force owners to re-justify or let them expire.

📚

Policy Templates

Mend ships out-of-the-box templates for common profiles: Strict Production, Internal Tools, Open Source Contributions, and Regulated Workloads. Templates give new teams a defensible starting point instead of building policy from scratch.

🛡

Enforcement Modes

Each rule can be set to Block (fail the build), Warn (alert but proceed), or Report Only (collect data without disrupting flow). Roll out new policies in Report Only mode, observe impact, then escalate to Block once teams are ready.

Compliance Mapping & Governance Reporting

Policy enforcement is only valuable if you can prove it to auditors and executives. Mend maps each policy rule to one or more controls in common compliance frameworks — OWASP ASVS, NIST SSDF, PCI DSS, ISO 27001, SOC 2, and the EU Cyber Resilience Act. When a build passes a policy gate, Mend records the corresponding control as satisfied for that artifact and produces evidence artifacts that can be handed directly to an auditor.

The governance reporting engine produces three classes of output. Policy compliance reports show, per project, what percentage of builds satisfied every applicable rule and which rules generated the most violations. Exception reports enumerate every active override, its owner, and its age, giving governance committees a clear view of where the organization has deviated from baseline. Trend reports track policy compliance over time, making it possible to demonstrate to leadership that security posture is improving rather than degrading.

⚠️ Common Pitfall

Do not map every policy rule to every framework. Over-mapping dilutes the audit trail and makes evidence packages unwieldy. Map only the rules that genuinely satisfy a control, and document the rationale in the policy description field so auditors understand the linkage.
