Pricing & Licensing Models

How Mend.io Prices the Platform

Mend.io is sold as an annual SaaS subscription, and the primary unit of value is the active developer — the number of engineers whose code is protected by the platform. This developer-based model aligns cost with the size of the engineering organization rather than the number of scans, repositories, or lines of code, which keeps pricing predictable as customers grow their codebase or scan frequency.

The platform is modular. Customers can license Mend SCA (software composition analysis), Mend SAST (static application security testing), Mend Container, and Mend AppSec Platform capabilities independently, or bundle them together. Each product is priced against the same active-developer count, so a customer with 500 developers who adopts both SCA and SAST is licensing 500 developers on each product, not 1,000 seats.

Understanding this structure matters in every deal. When a prospect asks "what will this cost," the first discovery question is always the size of the engineering team and which products they need. From there you can model the annual contract value (ACV) accurately and avoid the common mistake of quoting per-repository or per-scan pricing that does not reflect how Mend sells.

Subscription vs. Consumption-Based Models

The dominant commercial model at Mend.io is the committed annual subscription, where the customer commits to a developer count for the year and pays a fixed fee. This gives both parties budget certainty: the customer knows exactly what they will spend, and Mend can forecast recurring revenue. Subscription is the right fit for the vast majority of enterprise buyers who value predictability and are running continuous scanning across an established engineering organization.

A consumption-based approach charges according to actual usage — for example, the number of developers actively scanning in a given period. Consumption models can appeal to organizations with highly variable headcount, seasonal contractor surges, or early-stage teams that want to start small and scale. The tradeoff is less budget predictability and the risk of bill shock during peak periods.

When positioning, lead with subscription for its predictability and the discounting leverage it unlocks. Reserve consumption framing for prospects who explicitly cannot commit to a fixed number, and always model both so the customer can see the cost curve. In most enterprise deals, a committed subscription with a modest growth allowance is both cheaper for the customer and more valuable for Mend than pure consumption.

💡 Positioning Tip

Frame the committed subscription as "insurance against price increases." Committing to a developer count for 12–36 months locks the per-developer rate, while consumption exposes the customer to list-price changes and usage spikes. This reframing turns a commitment ask into a customer benefit.

Bundling, Co-Terming, and Discount Structures

Multi-product bundles are the single most effective lever for expanding ACV while delivering more value to the customer. When a prospect licenses SCA, SAST, and Container together, they receive a bundle discount off the sum of the individual product prices — typically increasing with the number of products and the developer count. Bundles also improve stickiness: a customer running three integrated Mend products is far less likely to churn than one running a single point tool.

Co-terming aligns the end dates of multiple products or add-ons onto a single renewal date. If a customer buys SAST six months after their initial SCA purchase, co-terming pro-rates the SAST cost so both expire on the same day. This simplifies procurement, creates one clean renewal conversation, and prevents the operational drag of tracking multiple staggered renewals.

Discounting follows a few consistent principles. Larger developer commitments earn deeper per-developer discounts. Multi-year terms unlock additional discount in exchange for the longer commitment. And bundle discounts stack on top of volume discounts. Always build the quote so the customer can see the "à la carte" price next to the bundled price — the delta is your value story.

Renewals, Upsell, and Expansion

Recurring revenue lives or dies at renewal, so treat the renewal cycle as a year-long motion, not a 30-day scramble. The strongest renewals are built on demonstrated value: track how many vulnerabilities the customer has remediated, how much developer time was saved through automated remediation, and how the platform supported audits or compliance. Bring this evidence to a quarterly business review so that by the time renewal arrives, the outcome is a foregone conclusion.

Expansion opportunities cluster around a few predictable triggers. Growth in the engineering team raises the developer count. Adoption of a second or third product expands the bundle. New requirements — a compliance mandate, a move to containers, an AI-code initiative — open the door to additional modules. And organizations that started in one business unit almost always have adjacent teams that can be brought onto the platform.

The healthiest accounts follow a "land and expand" arc: an initial deployment proves value, usage and satisfaction climb, and each renewal steps up in developer count and product breadth. Your job is to instrument that arc with clear success metrics so expansion is a natural continuation of value rather than a hard new sell.

  • Seat Expansion — Engineering headcount growth increases the active-developer count at renewal
  • Cross-Sell — Add SAST or Container to an existing SCA customer to grow the bundle
  • New Initiatives — Compliance mandates, container adoption, or AI-code security open new modules
  • Business-Unit Land-and-Expand — Extend a proven deployment to adjacent teams and divisions
  • Multi-Year Uplift — Convert an annual customer to a multi-year term to lock retention
